Principal Hardware & Software Engineer · Remote only · Washington State

My background runs through Soviet aerospace education, decades of software work, Verilog and assembler, FPGA signal and protocol paths, browser-GPU systems, and physical-design attempts. Across those fields, the recurring move is to stop treating the inherited stack as final and reconstruct the mechanism where timing, state, routing, gain, or physical constraint actually lives.

Greenforest I/O is my public technical work site. It is not a forestry, landscaping, arborist, timber, or environmental-field-services business.

What I Contribute

I create escape routes from inherited technical stacks. When a conventional implementation hides the decisive constraint, I rebuild that boundary as an explicit circuit, shader pipeline, reconfigurable fabric, signal path, test, or process argument and carry it far enough for another engineer to run, challenge, and extend.

The bubbles-free bit-serial multiplier makes arithmetic scheduling visible. The FPGA systems work follows signals from thresholded RF and RMII Ethernet frames through quadrature logic, packet state, RAM, and adjacent-device configuration pins. The WebGL and GLSL work treats browser textures and framebuffer passes as direct computing machinery. The Cartilage learning path connects LUTs, drivers, distribution, state, timing, metal, composition, and runtime spatial reconfiguration before pointing to the current bounded implementation evidence.

What Connects The Work

The recurring question is not only whether a system can be made inspectable. It is whether a complete computational mechanism can be reconstructed from physically elementary pieces: local ownership, switching, routing, restoration, gain, fanout, state, configuration, and repeatable process.

That question shows up as serial arithmetic, one-pin radio, direct Ethernet and ARP logic, cross-device FPGA reconfiguration, physical MUX tiles, browser-GPU cellular automata, Cartilage reconfiguration mechanisms, bounded Transformer runs, wafer-diced chiplet research, and magnetic circuits. The topics are connected by the attempt to rebuild computation across abstraction boundaries instead of accepting those boundaries as fixed.

Why The Site Is Public

A stranger should not have to trust my taste to evaluate the work. The site keeps demos, source links, measured counts, images, runs, articles, and older source trails visible enough that a technical reader can separate working artifacts from still-open research.

The public record also shows the material condition behind the program: some of the next mechanisms need funding, collaborators, lab access, fabrication access, or an institution willing to treat the work as serious R&D rather than a private notebook.
