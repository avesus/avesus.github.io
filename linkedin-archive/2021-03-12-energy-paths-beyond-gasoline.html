Energy Paths Beyond Gasoline

Gasoline and diesel are difficult to replace because they combine energy density, storage, distribution, fast refueling, and mature engines. A replacement path has to match enough of that behavior to be useful.

Hydrogen is one candidate energy carrier. It can connect renewable generation, storage, fuel cells, turbines, and industrial heat, but it brings compression or liquefaction cost, distribution complexity, leakage, safety, and infrastructure questions.

Solar can produce electricity, and that electricity can produce hydrogen, but the full system still has to pay for land or water use, intermittency, storage, transmission, conversion losses, and delivery.

Geothermal, molten-salt nuclear, and future fusion belong in the same conversation because they address continuous heat and power rather than only vehicle batteries. Each path has its own engineering, regulatory, fuel-cycle, and deployment constraints.

The useful question is not which label sounds greenest. It is which path can deliver vehicle power, industrial heat, and building heat with a complete production and distribution chain.
