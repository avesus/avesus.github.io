A foundations note about classical logic, intuitionistic logic, axiom of choice, Univalent Foundations, Category Theory, and what changes when classicality is rejected.

LinkedIn postPosted 2020-09-01.

The Classicality Boundary

Constructive foundations change what a proof is allowed to mean. Rejecting some classical principles is not a cosmetic preference: it affects existence claims, excluded middle, choice principles, and what can be extracted as computation.

That can be powerful, especially when proof terms and construction matter. It also changes the contract for readers who expect ordinary classical reasoning. A foundations program should make that boundary explicit instead of treating it as a minor implementation detail.

The Useful Caution

Univalent Foundations, Category Theory, intuitionistic logic, and classical set-theoretic habits can all be serious tools. The warning is about substituting allegiance for analysis. If a foundation changes the rules of existence, equality, or choice, the change should be named plainly and defended at the point where it affects real mathematics.
