The Exchange

An exchange is not complete because effort was spent. It is complete when the other person receives something that works for their situation: a repair, a decision, a migration, a design review, a safer upgrade, a working feature, or a finished artifact.

That is why terms matter. The work, price, timing, limits, and deliverable should be clear enough that both sides can tell whether the promise was kept.

What The Other Person Receives

Useful delivery is specific. A production fix should leave behind a test or safer path. A migration should protect old records. An upgrade should preserve user settings. A technical review should make the next engineering decision easier. A repair should return the object in a state the owner can trust.

The value is not the seller's internal effort. The value is the improved situation on the buyer's side of the exchange.

Clear Terms

Clear terms protect both sides. They say what is included, what is not included, what counts as done, what is unknown, and what happens if the facts change.

This is not bureaucracy. It is part of the work. A vague promise can make even good technical effort hard to receive.

Trust

Trust is protected in practical ways: show the working result, name the limit, return bad news early, keep the artifact accessible, preserve the user's state, and do not hide the failure mode that matters.

Good exchange leaves the other person with more control over their own situation, not just a receipt.
