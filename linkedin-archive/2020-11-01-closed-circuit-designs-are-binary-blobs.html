The common thing between AMD and Intel is that the products they make has a very extensive use of IP: the proprietary circuit designs which small vendors can't use / are not aware of / prohibited by patents / costly to obtain. For open-source community, closed circuit designs are like closed source code: proprietary binary firmware blobs of hidden disaster to happen. FPGAs have enabled many practitioners to share VHDL and Verilog source codes of their circuits in open-sourced way, publicly, online, on GitHub. The fact that the two major open-circuit enabling companies (Altera and Xilinx) recently got absorbed by the two closed-circuit profit making companies is alarming for open world. Will Lattice Semiconductor withstand the harsh "fight" for openness? The best day will be when they publicly declare (at least a moral) support to the amazing IceStorm and Yosys communities!

Software openness has a familiar source-versus-binary distinction. Hardware has a similar boundary: can the circuit be read, modified, built, and taught, or is the useful design locked behind proprietary IP?

LinkedIn postPosted 2020-11-01.

Circuit Source Matters

A proprietary circuit block can be as opaque to a builder as a binary firmware blob is to a software developer. The problem is not only licensing. It is the loss of inspectability, modification, teaching, repair, and independent implementation.

FPGAs made a different habit possible. VHDL and Verilog can be shared, reviewed, simulated, synthesized, and improved in public. Open toolchains such as Yosys and Project IceStorm made that habit more practical for small devices and independent builders.

The Industry Boundary

Large semiconductor vendors depend on proprietary IP portfolios, patent position, and closed implementation details. Open hardware needs enough viable devices, documentation, and tooling that public HDL can still turn into working circuits without depending on a closed black box at the most important step.
