"A grain of dust one micrometer across, after being illuminated by the sun for just one microsecond, will have its location imprinted about 100 million times in the scattered photons. It’s because of this redundancy that objective, classical-like properties exist at all. Ten observers can each measure the position of a dust grain and find that it’s in the same location, because each can access a distinct replica of the information. In this view, we can assign an objective “position” to the speck not because it “has” such a position (whatever that means) but because its position state can imprint many identical replicas in the environment, so that different observers can reach a consensus." https://lnkd.in/g9bhQ44i

Redundant Records

The linked article describes the central quantum-Darwinism idea: a small object under illumination can leave many copies of position information in scattered photons. Observers do not need to disturb the object directly if separate environmental fragments already carry matching records.

That redundancy is the mechanism being proposed for objective, classical-looking properties. A position becomes broadly available because the environment has copied enough compatible information for multiple observers to agree.

The Useful Question

The interesting point is not the word "objective" by itself. The useful question is how many environmental records are produced, how stable they are, what information they preserve, and where the quantum-to-classical approximation breaks down.
