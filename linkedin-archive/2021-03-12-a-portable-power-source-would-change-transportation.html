Portable Power and Transportation Energy

A useful transportation fuel is not only an energy source. It is portable, compact, quickly distributed, affordable, and able to deliver high continuous power.

The hard replacement question is the continuous-power case: kilowatt-scale electric output in a package small enough to multiply across vehicles, trucks, and working equipment.

Battery vehicles can be practical in many use cases, but a full replacement of gasoline and diesel also asks for storage, charging capacity, land, grid capacity, logistics, materials, and downtime. Those constraints do not disappear because the motor is electric.

A compact 2 kW-class source that could run for long periods would change the economics of transport. The important question is not whether the source is chemical, nuclear, solar-assisted, or something else. The important question is whether it can meet power, mass, cost, safety, refill or recharge, and manufacturing constraints at transportation scale.

This is the engineering gap the original note was pointing at: replacing liquid fuel means replacing its delivered behavior, not only replacing the engine.
