# Inventor Is Not a Rank

*March 30–July 19, 2022*

Inventor is who you are.

It is okay to let people know you.

There is nothing too special or important about the word. The world is full of inventors. Some work inside corporations. Some work independently. Some hold patents. Some repair a mechanism, change a process, or build one tool that did not exist yesterday.

The word becomes embarrassing only when it is treated as a crown.

I use it as a job description for a kind of attention: I keep seeing things that should exist and cannot leave the gap alone.

## The title does not prove the work

Calling myself an inventor does not prove that an invention works.

A schematic is not a device. A device is not a reliable process. A prototype is not a product. A surprising idea is not automatically novel. A private result is not public evidence.

Those distinctions protect the word from becoming grandiose. They also protect the work from being hidden until it satisfies an impossible standard.

I can say:

- I am inventing this mechanism.
- This version is a concept.
- This part runs.
- This measurement failed.
- This claim remains untested.
- This is the boundary I have proved.

Identity supplies continuity. Evidence supplies credibility. They do not need to impersonate each other.

## Corporate and independent invention are different environments

A corporate inventor has colleagues, equipment, schedules, legal support, product context, and a reason for others to care about a narrow result. The same environment may constrain publication, ownership, direction, and time.

An independent inventor has freedom to cross fields and follow a strange mechanism. The cost is fragmentation. One person becomes circuit designer, programmer, machinist, writer, photographer, tester, publisher, recruiter, and source of morale.

The problem is not that independent people are less serious. It is that seriousness lacks infrastructure.

After work, people often retreat into separate “fun projects,” then gather to present their isolated creations. We call that community, but it rarely gives the project what a working team supplies every day: another person who remembers the unresolved problem.

What can one person tinker out? Quite a lot. The better question is what does not need to remain a one-person project.

## Stop networking; establish recurrence

A useful inventor community is not a crowd and not an audience.

It is a recurring workshop.

Participants do not need to share one employer or one grand objective. They need continuity:

- a regular meeting;
- a compact record of current work;
- artifacts brought into the room;
- explicit requests for help;
- commitments small enough to keep;
- permission to report failure;
- a way for newcomers to enter through useful work.

The meeting is not an event designed to entertain. It is an insider happening in the healthiest sense: people return because unfinished work belongs to the group’s memory.

One person brings a board. Another can write the testbench. Another notices a fabrication constraint. Another turns the result into a diagram. Collaboration begins from the edges of real objects.

## Share the personal story, not a heroic myth

An invention without a story becomes a feature list. A story without an artifact becomes branding.

The useful story explains why I could not stop thinking about the problem, what I tried, what changed my mind, and which part became real. It includes the period where I forgot the project, chased another field, or built the wrong machine. Those are not embarrassing interruptions. They reveal the actual selection process.

The story should not pretend destiny. I did not know which route would survive. The reader gains more from the discarded alternatives than from a polished claim that the final mechanism was obvious all along.

This is why inventors should write before a product launch. Writing makes the model transferable. It lets another person disagree with a specific mechanism rather than react to a slogan.

## Make the opportunity visible

People cannot join work they cannot see.

An independent project needs a clear surface:

- what exists now;
- what question it answers;
- what remains uncertain;
- what artifact can be inspected;
- what kind of contribution would change the result;
- how ownership and credit will work.

“I need help” is too broad. “I need someone to compare these two clock-distribution traces” is an invitation.

The same precision prevents accidental recruitment theater. A person should know whether they are being asked for a conversation, a volunteer experiment, paid work, a coauthored paper, or a long-term partnership.

## An ordinary identity with uncommon persistence

Inventor is not a protected rank above engineer, scientist, artist, technician, or user.

It is often all of them in sequence.

I may invent inside a company and build independently at night. I may discover that my proposed invention already exists and become its student. I may publish a failed route so the next person begins farther ahead.

The identity does not promise victory. It gives me permission to keep the problem coherent across those changes.

So yes: let people know.

Not because “inventor” makes me important. Because the work needs to find the other people who recognize the gap—and might finally stop tinkering alone.
